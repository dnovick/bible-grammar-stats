# Greek Words for "Obey" — NT and LXX Word Study

## Overview

The KJV translates several distinct Greek words as "obey," "obeyed," or "obedience."
This report documents every NT occurrence, the underlying Greek lemma and morphology,
and then traces those same lemmas through the LXX to identify their Hebrew roots.

**Greek lemmas covered:**

| Lemma | Strongs | Gloss | NT count | LXX count (canonical OT) |
|---|---|---|---:|---:|
| ὑπακούω | G5219 | to obey / hearken | 15 | 52 |
| ὑπακοή | G5218 | obedience (noun) | 1 | — |
| πείθω | G3982 | to persuade / comply | 7 | 131 |
| πειθαρχέω | G3980 | to obey authority | 3 | — |
| ὑποτάσσω | G5293 | to subject / subordinate | 1 | — |
| ὑπήκοος | G5255 | obedient (adjective) | 1 | — |

**Key finding:** The NT uses at least 5 distinct Greek words where the KJV says "obey."
The most important distinction is between **ὑπακούω** (responsive hearing/compliance)
and **πείθω** (being persuaded/confident). In the LXX, πείθω almost exclusively
translates **בָּטַח** (*to trust, be confident*) — not an obedience word at all.

### πείθω (G3982) — *to persuade* (passive: to comply / be persuaded) — 7 NT instances

Root meaning is *to persuade*. In the passive/middle it shades into *being persuaded*,
which the KJV renders as "obey." This is a weaker, more relational nuance than ὑπακούω —
compliance arising from persuasion rather than responsive hearing.
Note that Heb 13:17 is the only NT Imperative use: the command to *be persuaded by* leaders.

### πειθαρχέω (G3980) — *to obey authority* — 3 NT instances

A compound of πείθω + ἀρχή (authority/ruler). More specific than ὑπακούω:
obedience specifically to constituted authority (God, magistrates).
All three NT instances are in Acts 5 and Titus 3, both contexts of civil/divine authority.

## Part 2 — LXX (Septuagint, Canonical OT)

### ὑπακούω (G5219) in the LXX — 52 instances

in contexts of obedience to God's voice, a parent's voice, or a master's call.
The Hebrew behind it is typically **שָׁמַע** (*to hear/hearken*), frequently in the
formula "hearken to/obey my voice" (שָׁמַע בְּקוֹל).

In Job, ὑπακούω shifts toward "to answer" — responding to a call or challenge —
reflecting the broader semantic range of שָׁמַע.

### πείθω (G3982) in the LXX — Hebrew root בָּטַח

Where the IBM Model 1 word alignment identifies a Hebrew source for LXX πείθω,
it is **almost exclusively בָּטַח** (*to trust, be secure, be confident*) —
not an obedience word. The LXX translators used πείθω in its **perfect tense**
to render the Hebrew stative: the state of *being confident / trusting*.

This is a critical observation for NT interpretation: πείθω enters the NT's
obedience vocabulary carrying an LXX background of **trust and confidence**,
not compliance. When Paul uses πείθω passively in obedience contexts
(Gal 3:1; 5:7; Heb 13:17), the nuance is *be persuaded / trust* rather than
*submit / comply*.

## Part 3 — Key Observations

### 1. ὑπακούω is the primary NT obedience word
15 of 28 KJV "obey" instances in the NT use ὑπακούω. It is always active voice,
always directed at a specific voice or command. Its LXX background (52 occurrences)
confirms it carries the Hebrew concept of *hearkening to a voice* (שָׁמַע בְּקוֹל).

### 2. πείθω ≠ obey in the LXX
πείθω appears 131 times in the canonical LXX, but where a Hebrew root can be aligned,
it almost always translates **בָּטַח** (*to trust, be secure*) — not any Hebrew
obedience word. The LXX translators used the *perfect tense* of πείθω to express
the resultant state of *being confident*. This means NT uses of πείθω passively
("be persuaded," rendered "obey") carry an undertone of **trust and confidence**
rather than simple compliance.

### 3. Titus 3:1 uses two different obedience words
The KJV renders Titus 3:1 as "obey magistrates" twice, but the Greek uses
**ὑποτάσσω** ("be subject to, subordinate oneself to authorities") followed by
**πειθαρχέω** ("obey authority") — two distinct concepts in one verse.

### 4. The Imperative mood is reserved for specific contexts
Obedience commands (Imperative mood) appear in:
- **Household codes:** ὑπακούω Imperative — children/servants to parents/masters (Eph 6:1; Col 3:20, 22)
- **Church leadership:** πείθω Passive Imperative — be persuaded by your leaders (Heb 13:17)

The Synoptic nature miracles (wind and sea obeying) are all **Present Indicative** —
describing ongoing natural submission, not commanding it.

### 5. Zephaniah 3:2 — the clearest single-verse illustration
*"She obeyed not the voice [ὑπακούω]; she received not correction;
she trusted not [πείθω / בָּטַח] in the Lord; she drew not near to her God."*

This verse uses both ὑπακούω and πείθω in the same breath to describe Jerusalem's
failure — perfectly illustrating how the LXX distinguishes responsive hearing
(ὑπακούω) from confident trust (πείθω/בָּטַח).
