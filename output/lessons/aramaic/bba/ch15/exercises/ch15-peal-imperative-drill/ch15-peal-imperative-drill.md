## Reflection Questions

1. Items 1 (קוּם) and 3 (כְּתֻב) are both 2ms imperatives, but item 1 has a long ū vowel throughout while item 3 has a shewa under the first radical. What accounts for this difference in initial vocalization, and what root type is each?

2. Item 2 (אֱמַר) has an initial aleph with hateph-seghol (אֱ-). Is this the root aleph of אמר, or something else? Why does this form not look like the standard strong-verb imperative pattern (קְטֻל)?

3. Items 7 (הַב) and 10 (הָבוּ) come from the root יהב. Where did the initial yod go? Compare: the Peal imperfect of יהב is יִנְתֵּן (suppletive). Does the imperative also use a suppletive form? What does this tell you about how the imperative and imperfect of יהב are related?

4. Item 9 (בְּנוֹ) is the 2mp imperative of בנה (to build). The strong-verb 2mp imperative ends in **וּ-** (shureq), but this form ends in **וֹ-** (holem). What root class is בנה, and why does the 2mp imperative of III-he roots end differently?

5. Items 17–20 are all prohibitions. Items 17 and 19 use אַל; items 18 and 20 use לָא. Both are followed by an imperfect form, not an imperative. Explain the difference in usage between אַל + imperfect (jussive) and לָא + imperfect for expressing "Do not."

### Answers to Reflection Questions

**1.** Item 1 (קוּם) is a **hollow verb** (root קום, II-waw). Hollow verb imperatives do not begin with a two-consonant cluster because the first and third radicals are separated by the long ū vowel (shureq) — no shewa is needed under the first radical. The form קוּם is derived from the 2ms imperfect תְּקוּם by removing the prefix: the long vowel ū already makes the word pronounceable. Item 3 (כְּתֻב) is a **strong root** (כתב). After removing the prefix from תִּכְתֻּב, the first two consonants (כ and ת) are left without an intervening vowel, so a **vocal shewa** (שְׁוָא נָע) is inserted under the first consonant: כְּ-תֻב. The shewa is the standard resolution for initial clusters in strong-root imperatives.

**2.** The אֱ- in אֱמַר is **not** the root aleph of אמר — it is a **prosthetic aleph with hateph-seghol**, added to make the word pronounceable. The root aleph of אמר is the second consonant in the form (the מ follows it directly: אֱ + מַר). When the imperfect prefix (תֵּ-) is removed from the 2ms imperfect תֵּאמַר, one arrives at a form that would begin with aleph + mem — but the aleph here cannot carry the load of an initial consonant in isolation. Aramaic resolves this with the prosthetic אֱ prefix. Additionally, the I-aleph root class uses an **a-class (patach) stem vowel** (מַ) rather than the u-class qibbuts of strong roots — because the root aleph (a guttural) favors a-class vowels in the stem.

**3.** In the Peal imperative, the initial **yod of root יהב drops** — a standard behavior for I-yod roots in both Hebrew and Aramaic imperatives (compare Hebrew: נָתַן → תֵּן; similarly for certain I-waw roots). The imperative does **not** use the suppletive form יִנְתֵּן — that suppletive belongs only to the imperfect. The imperative returns to the original יהב root, strips the yod, and produces הַב (2ms) / הָבוּ (2mp). This shows that the suppletive relationship is imperfect-specific: perfect = יהב root; imperfect = נתן-derived suppletive; imperative = יהב root (minus yod).

**4.** Root בנה is a **III-he verb** (final radical he). In strong verbs, the 2mp imperative suffix is **וּ-** (shureq): כְּתֻבוּ. In III-he verbs, the final he of the root interacts with the suffix: the he drops and the preceding vowel shifts. The result for 2mp is the suffix **וֹ-** (holem), producing בְּנוֹ rather than *בְּנוּ. This **holem 2mp ending** is the diagnostic of III-he imperatives in the masculine plural. Compare: III-he 2mp imperative בְּנוֹ vs. strong verb 2mp imperative כְּתֻבוּ.

**5.** Both constructions prohibit action, but they differ in **urgency and scope**:
- **אַל + jussive (3rd-person imperfect)** (items 17, 19): expresses an **urgent, immediate prohibition** — "Stop!" or "Don't do that right now!" It is the more vivid, energetic form, used in dramatic narrative moments (e.g., an angel saying "Do not be afraid!" or a bystander warning against an imminent act). The verb form after אַל is technically a jussive (morphologically identical to the imperfect in Aramaic), giving the meaning "let it not be that you do X."
- **לָא + imperfect** (items 18, 20): expresses a **general or categorical prohibition** — a standing rule, law, or decree. It is the construction of legislation and royal edicts: "You shall not bow down to the statue" is not a warning for a single moment but a standing prohibition. This parallels Hebrew לֹא + imperfect in legal texts (e.g., the Ten Commandments).
