# Chapter 21 — Haphel Stem Drill

*Basics of Biblical Aramaic, Van Pelt — Ch21: The Haphel Stem*

---

## Instructions

For each numbered verb form drawn from Daniel or Ezra, provide:

1. **Conjugation** — Perfect, Imperfect, Imperative, Infinitive, or Participle (active)
2. **Root** — the three root consonants (cite in Peal perfect 3ms form)
3. **PGN** — Person-Gender-Number where applicable (N/A for infinitive; ms/mp/fs/fp for participle)
4. **Translation** — an English rendering appropriate to the stem and conjugation

All forms in this drill are **Haphel** (H stem — causative).

**Quick diagnostics:**
- **Haphel perfect:** הַ- prefix before R1; tsere under R2; no R2 dagesh
- **Haphel imperfect:** יְ- prefix letter + הַ visible in stem (יְהַ-); tsere in R2
- **Haphel imperative:** same as perfect form (הַ- prefix); context distinguishes
- **Haphel infinitive:** לְהַ-/לְהוֹ- + stem + -ָה ending; qamets under R2
- **Haphel participle:** מְהַ- prefix; tsere in R2; no R2 dagesh (contrast Pael: מְ- + R2 dagesh)
- **Weak root notes:**
  - I-yod roots (ידע): הַ + יְ → הוֹ- prefix → **הוֹדַע** pattern
  - Hollow roots (קום): הַ + root contraction → **הַקִּים** pattern (dagesh here = compensatory, not D-stem)
  - I-aleph/I-yod "bring" root (יתי/אתה): fixed stem **הֵיתִי** (perfect 3ms)

## Reflection Questions

1. Items 1 (הוֹדַע) and 4 (יְהוֹדַע) both come from root ידע and both are Haphel, but different conjugations. (a) How does the Haphel prefix appear differently in the perfect vs. the imperfect? (b) What phonological process explains the הוֹ- sequence in both forms?

2. Item 2 (הַקִּים) has a dagesh forte in מ, yet this is Haphel (not Pael). Why does the dagesh appear, and how does it differ in origin and function from the D-stem dagesh in the Pael? What root is this, and what class of weak root?

3. Item 6 (מְהוֹדֵעַ) and item 20 (מְהַקְטֵל) are both Haphel participles but from different root types. (a) What prefix marks both as participles? (b) How does the weak root ידע affect the appearance of the Haphel marker in item 6 compared to item 20?

4. Compare items 8 (מְהַעְדֵּה) and 9 (מְהַשְׁנֵא). Both are Haphel participles of III-he roots. What is the III-he ending on the participle? How would these forms look if the roots were strong (e.g., מְהַקְטֵל)?

5. Items 13 (הֵיתִי) and 3 (הֵיתִיוּ) are from the same root and stem. What is the root, and why does this Haphel form look so different from a typical Haphel perfect like הוֹדַע or הַקִּים? What morphological elements can you identify in הֵיתִי?

### Answers to Reflection Questions

**1.** (a) In the **perfect** (הוֹדַע), the Haphel prefix appears as part of the stem itself, fused with the weak root — the form begins with הוֹ- directly. In the **imperfect** (יְהוֹדַע), the imperfect prefix letter יְ- stands before the stem, and the Haphel marker (here appearing as הוֹ- due to weak root) follows inside the stem. So the imperfect adds יְ- before the Haphel element. (b) The root ידע is **I-yod**: its first consonant is yod (י). When the Haphel prefix הַ- is placed before a I-yod root, the patach vowel of הַ- and the yod of the root contract: הַ + יְ → the yod weakens and the vowel lengthens to **holem-waw** (הוֹ-). This produces the characteristic **הוֹ-** sequence seen in both the perfect (הוֹדַע) and the imperfect stem (יְהוֹדַע).

**2.** The dagesh forte in מ of **הַקִּים** is a **compensatory dagesh** (or **contraction dagesh**), not the D-stem doubling dagesh. The root is **קום**, a **hollow root (II-waw)**: R2 is waw (ו). When the Haphel prefix הַ- is applied to a hollow root, the middle radical (waw) weakens or contracts, and the resulting shortening of the syllable is compensated by dagesh forte in the following consonant. This dagesh marks the phonological **contraction of the hollow root**, not the intensification/doubling function of the D stem. In the Pael, dagesh in R2 **defines the stem** (D-stem doubling = semantic marker); in הַקִּים, the dagesh in מ (R3) is a **phonological by-product** of root contraction. The Haphel is the H stem and never has D-stem doubling of R2.

**3.** (a) Both **מְהוֹדֵעַ** and **מְהַקְטֵל** begin with **מְ-** (mem + shewa) — the universal derived-stem participle prefix in Biblical Aramaic. (b) In item 6 (מְהוֹדֵעַ, root ידע), the I-yod weak root causes the same הַ + יְ → הוֹ- contraction seen in the perfect and imperfect. So the Haphel marker appears as **מְהוֹ-** (mem + shewa + heh + holem-waw) rather than the transparent **מְהַ-** (mem + shewa + heh + patach) of item 20 (מְהַקְטֵל, model strong root). The underlying Haphel marker הַ is present in both, but the I-yod contraction masks it in item 6, whereas item 20 shows the Haphel marker undisguised.

**4.** Both **מְהַעְדֵּה** (root עדה) and **מְהַשְׁנֵא** (root שׁנה) end in **-ֵה** or **-ֵא** — the characteristic **III-he/III-aleph ending** for the ms participle in derived stems. Strong roots form the Haphel ms participle as מְהַקְטֵל (ending in consonant + tsere); III-he roots replace R3 with the weak ending **-ֵה** (or -ֵא with III-aleph). So **מְהַעְדֵּה** = מְהַ- + עְדֵּ + ה (R3 as heh); **מְהַשְׁנֵא** = מְהַ- + שְׁנֵ + א (R3 as aleph). Both show the same phenomenon: the third radical weakens to a quiescent or final letter, leaving the characteristic tsere + weak consonant ending.

**5.** The root is **יתי/אתה** ("to come"). The Haphel of this verb means "to bring / to cause to come." The form **הֵיתִי** looks different from other Haphel perfects because of the double weakness of the root — it is both **I-aleph/I-yod** (the initial root consonant is a weak letter) and has an unusual base form. The prefix הַ- interacts with the initial י/א to produce **הֵי-** (heh + tsere + yod) rather than הַ- + root consonant. The full form הֵיתִי = **הֵי-** (Haphel prefix + weak R1) + **תִ** (R2) + **י** (III-he 3ms ending or stem ending). This form should be memorized as a lexical item: **הֵיתִי** = Haphel perfect 3ms, "he brought."
