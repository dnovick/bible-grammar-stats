# Chapter 18 — Peil and Ithpeel Stem Drill

*Basics of Biblical Aramaic, Van Pelt — Ch18: The Peil, Hithpeel, and Ithpeel Stems*

---

## Instructions

For each numbered verb form drawn from Daniel or Ezra, provide:

1. **Stem** — Peil (simple passive of Peal) or Ithpeel (reflexive/passive of Peal)
2. **Conjugation** — Perfect, Imperfect, or Participle
3. **Root** — the three root consonants (cite in Peal perfect 3ms form)
4. **Translation** — an English rendering appropriate to the stem and conjugation

**Quick diagnostics:**
- **Peil perfect:** hireq-yod (ִי) between R2 and R3; vocal shewa under R1 (e.g., רְמִי, כְּתִיב)
- **Peil imperfect:** standard prefix + vocal shewa on R1 + tsere on R2 (e.g., יִקְטֵל)
- **Ithpeel perfect:** הִתְ- or אִתְ- prefix before the stem (e.g., אִתְכְּנִישׁ)
- **Ithpeel imperfect:** יִתְ- (tāw after the prefix vowel) before R1 (e.g., יִתְקְטֵל)
- **Ithpeel participle:** מִתְ- prefix (e.g., מִתְכַּנְּשִׁין)
- **Metathesis:** when R1 is a sibilant (שׁ, שׂ, ס, צ), the tāw and R1 switch — look for הִשְׁתַּ- / יִשְׁתַּ-

## Reflection Questions

1. Items 1 (רְמִי) and 9 (הִשְׁתַּנִּי) are both Ithpeel/Peil perfects, but they look completely different. What specific features identify each as a derived-stem passive form, and which stem does each belong to?

2. Items 5 (הִשְׁתְּכַח) and 11 (יִשְׁתַּנֵּא) both involve metathesis. For each one: (a) identify the root, (b) explain which consonants switched positions, and (c) explain why the metathesis occurred.

3. Item 13 (מִתְכַּנְּשִׁין) is an Ithpeel participle. What does the prefix מִתְ- tell you about the form's stem and its verbal category? How would the same root appear in the Peal active participle?

4. Items 6 (כְּתִיב) and 15 (קְטִילוּ) both show the qəṭīl pattern. Item 6 could be parsed as either a Peil perfect 3ms or a Peal passive participle. What contextual clue would help you decide which parsing applies?

5. Items 3 (יִתְנְסַח) and 7 (יִתְבְּנֵא) are both Ithpeel imperfects. What shared morphological feature identifies them as Ithpeel, and how does the III-he root of item 7 affect the final form compared to item 3's strong root?

### Answers to Reflection Questions

**1.** Item 1 (רְמִי) is a **Peil perfect** identified by its qəṭīl-like pattern: vocal shewa under R1 (רְ-), hireq under R2 (מִ-), with the III-he root appearing as a final yod (-י) at R3. The Peil marker is the hireq-yod vowel pattern on R2 combined with its function as a finite passive predicate. Item 9 (הִשְׁתַּנִּי) is a **Hithpeel perfect** identified by the הִ- prefix vowel combined with the שׁת- cluster — recognizable as a metathesized Hithpeel. The הִ- is the prefix vowel of the Hithpeel, the שׁ is R1 of the root, and the תַּ- is the metathesized tāw. The doubled nun and final yod reflect the III-he root שׁנה. These two passives use completely different morphological strategies: the Peil encodes passivity through internal vowel change (hireq-yod on R2); the Hithpeel/Ithpeel encodes it through a prefixed element.

**2.** Item 5 (הִשְׁתְּכַח) — Root: **שׁכח** (shin-kaph-het, "to find/discover"). Metathesis: the normal Hithpeel would produce הִתְשְׁכַח (prefix הִתְ- + root שׁכח), but the tāw of הִתְ- and the initial שׁ of the root switched positions, yielding הִשְׁתְּ- (he-shin-tāw) instead of הִתְ-שׁ- (he-tāw-shin). The metathesis occurs because the initial root consonant (שׁ) is a **sibilant**, and Aramaic (like Hebrew) transposes the tāw prefix with an initial sibilant to produce a more pronounceable consonant cluster. Item 11 (יִשְׁתַּנֵּא) — Root: **שׁנה** (shin-nun-he, "to change"). Metathesis: the expected Ithpeel imperfect יִתְשְׁנֵא becomes יִשְׁתַּנֵּא — the יִ- (prefix vowel) remains, but תְ and שׁ switch, placing שׁ first (יִשְׁ-) and then תַּ (with dagesh/patach modification). The final נֵּא reflects the III-he root with the nun doubled (by assimilation of the final weak consonant before the suffix).

**3.** The prefix **מִתְ-** on item 13 (מִתְכַּנְּשִׁין) communicates two things simultaneously: the **מ-** is the universal Aramaic (and Hebrew) participle prefix for derived stems, indicating that the form is a participle (not a finite perfect or imperfect); the **תְ** is the Gt-stem marker (same tāw that appears in the Ithpeel perfect and imperfect prefixes), indicating the Ithpeel stem. Together, מִתְ- = "Ithpeel participle." The -ִין suffix (-īn) indicates masculine plural. The same root כנשׁ in the **Peal active participle** ms would be כָּנֵשׁ (qāṭēl pattern: qamets on R1, tsere on R2).

**4.** Item 6 (כְּתִיב) is formally ambiguous. The qəṭīl pattern (vocal shewa under כ, hireq-yod under ת) fits both the **Peil perfect 3ms** ("it was written" — a past passive event) and the **Peal passive participle ms** ("written" — a state or quality). To distinguish them, look at the syntactic context: if the form is making a finite assertion about a past event in a narrative sequence (e.g., "the decree was written and sealed"), treat it as Peil perfect. If the form is functioning as a predicate adjective in a verbless clause (e.g., "it is written in the law") or as an attributive modifier of a noun, treat it as the Peal passive participle. In the formulaic citation use — כְּתִיב = "it is written" introducing a quoted document — the form functions most like a stative/result-state passive participle, though the boundary with the Peil perfect is thin.

**5.** Both items 3 (יִתְנְסַח) and 7 (יִתְבְּנֵא) begin with **יִתְ-** — the Ithpeel imperfect prefix, which consists of the standard imperfect prefix vowel (יִ-) plus the Gt-stem tāw (תְ-) placed before R1 of the root. This יִתְ- cluster is the defining mark of the Ithpeel imperfect; no other stem places a tāw directly after the prefix vowel and before R1. Regarding the III-he root: item 3 has R1 = nun, R2 = samek, R3 = het — all strong consonants — so the form ends regularly in the consonant ח: יִתְנְסַח. Item 7 has a **III-he root** (בנה), so the historical third radical (he) does not appear as a full consonant in word-final position; instead the form ends in aleph (mater lectionis for the final ā vowel of the III-he imperfect): יִתְבְּנֵא. The -ēʾ ending (-ֵא) is the III-he imperfect ending throughout the Aramaic verb system, just as III-he imperfects in the Peal end in -ēʾ (e.g., יִבְנֵא, "he will build").
