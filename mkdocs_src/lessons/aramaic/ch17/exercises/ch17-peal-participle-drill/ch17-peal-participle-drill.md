## Reflection Questions

1. Items 1 (כָּתֵב) and 2 (כְּתִיב) come from the same root. What single feature of the vowel pattern tells you immediately which is active and which is passive? Describe the diagnostic without looking at the answer key.

2. Items 5 (קָאֵם) and 16 (נָפֵל) are both ms active participles, but from different weak root classes. What root class is each? What happens to the middle root letter in each case, and why do the patterns look so different from each other?

3. Items 12 (כְּתִיבָא) and 18 (כָּתְבַיָּא) are both in the determined state. One is passive ms determined; the other is active mp determined. What state-marking feature do they share, and how do you tell them apart?

4. Item 9 (חָזֵה) is a III-he active participle. What is unusual about the final consonant of this form compared to a regular strong verb active participle (like כָּתֵב)? What does the final he indicate?

5. In the periphrastic construction, the participle combines with a form of הֲוָה to express past progressive. Using item 3 (כָּתְבִין, mp active), write the full periphrastic expression for "they were writing" in Biblical Aramaic, including the correct form of הֲוָה.

### Answers to Reflection Questions

**1.** The single diagnostic is the **vowel pattern on R1 and R2**. Active: **qamets under R1 + tsere under R2** (קָ-טֵ-ל). Passive: **vocal shewa under R1 + hireq-yod under R2** (קְ-טִי-ל). So כָּתֵב has qamets (ā) on R1 + tsere (ē) on R2 → active. כְּתִיב has shewa (ə) on R1 + hireq-yod (ī) on R2 → passive. The shewa vs. qamets on R1 is the clearest visual cue: an active participle of a strong verb always opens with a qamets syllable; a passive participle opens with a reduced (shewa) syllable.

**2.** Item 5 (קָאֵם) is from the **hollow root** קום (II-waw). The middle radical (waw) is the weak letter. In the active participle, the hollow middle vowel appears as an aleph-tsere sequence (-אֵ-) standing in for the original internal long vowel of the root. The form has only two consonants in visible strong positions (ק-ם) with the middle position occupied by the mater aleph. Item 16 (נָפֵל) is from an **I-nun root** (נפל). The initial nun is retained as a regular first radical in the participle (unlike the imperfect, where it assimilates). So the form נָפֵל looks perfectly regular — qamets on נ, tsere on פ — because the nun does not cause any special behavior in the participle paradigm. The two forms look different because one (קָאֵם) has a weak *middle* consonant that produces the distinctive aleph-tsere pattern, while the other (נָפֵל) has a weak *initial* consonant that behaves regularly in the participle.

**3.** Both forms share the **-א** (aleph) suffix as the determined state marker for ms (כְּתִיבָא) and the **-יָּא** (-ayyā) suffix as the determined state marker for mp (כָּתְבַיָּא). These suffixes are the standard Aramaic determined state markers that replace the absolute-state endings: ms absolute → ms det. adds -ā; mp absolute (-ִין) → mp det. replaces -ין with -יָּא. To tell them apart: (a) The suffix itself distinguishes them — simple -א = singular; -יָּא = plural. (b) The stem vowels also differ: כְּתִיבָא has the qəṭīl passive pattern (shewa on R1, hireq-yod on R2) before the aleph suffix; כָּתְבַיָּא has the reduced active stem (qamets on R1 is reduced because of the following syllable) before the -יָּא suffix.

**4.** The strong verb active participle (like כָּתֵב) ends with a regular consonant in word-final position — R3 is simply the third consonant with no final vowel marker. The III-he form חָזֵה ends in **he (ה) as a mater lectionis** — a final "silent he" that marks the historical third radical of the root. The root חזה originally had ה as its third consonant. In the active participle, this historical he remains visible as a mater (vowel letter) at the end of the word. This is analogous to the way III-he verbs throughout the BBA curriculum (perfect: בְּנָה; imperfect: יִבְנֵה; inf.: מִבְנֵא) show a final vowel marker where strong verbs would have a consonant in word-final position.

**5.** The periphrastic expression for "they were writing" (3mp subject) requires the 3mp form of הֲוָה and the mp active participle. The 3mp imperfect of הֲוָה is **הֲווֹ** (or הֲוֹ). The mp active participle of כתב is **כָּתְבִין**. The full expression is:

> **הֲווֹ כָּתְבִין** — "they were writing"

Both forms agree with the subject in masculine plural. The form הֲווֹ is the 3mp (imperfect or perfect, depending on the context) of the verb הוה, which functions as the copula in this periphrastic construction.
