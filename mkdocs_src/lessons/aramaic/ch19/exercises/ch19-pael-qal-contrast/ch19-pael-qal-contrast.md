# Chapter 19 — Peal vs. Pael Contrast Drill

*Basics of Biblical Aramaic, Van Pelt — Ch19: Intensive/Causative Pael vs. Simple Peal*

---

## Instructions

For each numbered Aramaic verbal form drawn from Daniel or Ezra, provide:

1. **Stem** — Peal (simple) or Pael (intensive/factitive/causative)
2. **Conjugation** — Perfect, Imperfect, or Participle
3. **PGN** — person (1/2/3), gender (m/f/c), number (s/pl)
4. **Root** — the three root consonants
5. **Translation** — an English rendering that reflects the stem's meaning difference

**Quick diagnostics:**

- **Peal Perfect 3ms**: CvCaC — shewa under R1, patach under R2; no dagesh in R2 (e.g., כְּתַב, אֲמַר)
- **Pael Perfect 3ms**: CaCCēC — patach under R1, **dagesh forte in R2**, tsere under R2 (e.g., כַּתֵּב, שַׁלַּח); the dagesh in R2 is the key marker
- **Pael Imperfect 3ms**: יְ‑ prefix (shewa) + CaCCeC pattern (e.g., יְבַטֵּל)
- **Pael Participle ms**: מְ‑ prefix + CaCCeC (e.g., מְשַׁבַּח)
- **Summary**: dagesh forte in R2 = Pael; no dagesh in R2 = Peal

## Discussion Questions

1. The Pael is often described as the Aramaic equivalent of the Hebrew Piel. The diagnostic feature is the same: dagesh forte in R2. Compare items 1/2 (כְּתַב vs. כַּתֵּב) and 19/20 (שְׁלַח vs. שַׁלַּח). In item 20, does the Pael add an intensive nuance, a factitive nuance, or a causative nuance? Explain.

2. Item 6 (בַּטִּיל) uses the root בטל ("to cease"). The Peal would mean "to cease" intransitively; the Pael is transitive: "to cause to cease." This is the factitive function. Find two other Pael items in the list where the Pael is clearly causative/factitive rather than just intensive.

3. Item 18 (מְשַׁבַּח) is a Pael Participle. How do you distinguish a Pael Participle from a Pael Perfect 3ms? Write out the expected Pael Perfect 3ms of שׁבח and compare.

### Answers to Discussion Questions

**1.** In items 1/2 (כְּתַב vs. כַּתֵּב), the Pael adds an **intensive** or **thoroughness** nuance to the act of writing — it can suggest writing carefully, at length, or officially. In items 19/20 (שְׁלַח vs. שַׁלַּח), the Peal means "he sent" in the simple sense; the Pael (שַׁלַּח) means "he sent away" or "he released/dismissed." This is best classified as a **causative/factitive** nuance: the Pael takes an object that in the Peal would simply be the recipient and makes it the object of a transitivizing or releasing action. Daniel 5:13 uses שַׁלַּח in the sense of bringing someone in by command. The nuance here is not mere intensification but the introduction of a cause-and-effect relationship — "he caused to go."

**2.** Two other Pael items where the function is clearly **causative/factitive** rather than intensification of the same action:

- **Item 12** (רַבִּי from רבה): The Peal of רבה would mean "to be/become great." The Pael is transitive and factitive: "he made great" or "he caused to be great / magnified." The subject causes the object to enter the state of greatness.
- **Item 14** (כַּבֵּד from כבד): The Peal would mean "to be heavy/honored." The Pael is factitive: "he honored" — he caused the object to be in a state of honor. This is the classic factitive pattern: Pael causes the state expressed by the Peal stative.

**3.** The **Pael Participle ms** pattern is מְCaCCaC: a מְ‑ prefix (with shewa) followed by the Pael stem vowels. For item 18 (מְשַׁבַּח): מְ‑ prefix + שַׁבַּח (patach under R1, dagesh in R2, patach under R2). The **Pael Perfect 3ms** of the same root שׁבח would be **שַׁבַּח** — identical to the stem portion of the participle but without the מְ‑ prefix. The only distinction is the מְ‑ prefix, which converts the Perfect form into a Participle. This is why the prefix מְ‑ is the critical marker: מְשַׁבַּח = Participle (ongoing action); שַׁבַּח = Perfect 3ms (completed action).
