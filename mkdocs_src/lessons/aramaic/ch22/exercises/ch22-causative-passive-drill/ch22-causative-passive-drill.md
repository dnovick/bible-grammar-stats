## Reflection Questions

1. Items 1 (שֵׁיזִב), 14 (הוֹדַע), and 18 (הַקִּים) are all perfect 3ms forms. Two are Haphel and one is Shaphel. (a) What diagnostic feature identifies item 1 as Shaphel rather than Haphel? (b) Why does שֵׁיזִב show שֵׁי- rather than the expected שַׁ-? What type of root is involved?

2. Items 2 (הֻנְעַל) and 14 (הוֹדַע) both begin with הַ-/הֻ-. What single feature distinguishes the Hophal (item 2) from the Haphel (item 14), and what does that difference signal about the verb's function?

3. Item 3 (אַחֲוִי) is Aphel and item 16 (יְהַקְטֵל) is Haphel imperfect. (a) Both stems are causative. What morphological difference marks item 3 as Aphel and not Haphel? (b) In what sense are the Aphel and Haphel functionally equivalent?

4. Items 9 (כְּתַב), 10 (כְּתִיב), and 11 (אִתְכְּתִב) are all from the same root. What is the root? Name the stem of each form. What semantic relationship exists among these three stems?

5. Items 12 (קַטֵּל) and 19 (מְקַטֵּל) are both from the Pael. What is the difference in conjugation between them, and what single morphological feature is diagnostic for the Pael in both forms?

### Answers to Reflection Questions

**1.** (a) Item 1 (שֵׁיזִב) is identified as **Shaphel** because the first consonant is **שׁ** (shin), not **ה** (heh). The causative prefix is **שַׁ-/שֵׁ-** (shin + vowel), whereas the Haphel uses **הַ-** (heh + patach). The shin is the decisive morphological marker. (b) The expected Shaphel prefix is שַׁ- (shin + patach). In שֵׁיזִב, the patach has lengthened to **tsere** (שֵׁ-), and the syllable שֵׁי- appears because the root יזב is an **I-yod weak root** (R1 = yod). When the Shaphel prefix שַׁ- is placed before the I-yod R1, the yod causes the patach to lengthen to tsere and the yod is preserved in the שֵׁי- sequence — exactly parallel to how the Haphel prefix הַ- + I-yod produces הוֹ- (but with shin → tsere + yod rather than heh → holem-waw).

**2.** The single distinguishing feature is the **vowel under the heh**: **הַ-** (heh + patach = short a-vowel) identifies the **Haphel** (causative active), while **הֻ-** (heh + qibbuts = short u-vowel) identifies the **Hophal** (causative passive). Item 2 (הֻנְעַל) begins with הֻ-, signaling that this form is **passive** — the subject receives the causative action rather than performing it. Item 14 (הוֹדַע) has הוֹ- (from Haphel הַ- + I-yod contraction), signaling **active** causation. The Hophal/Haphel distinction in Biblical Aramaic mirrors the Hebrew Hophal (הֻ-) / Hiphil (הִ-) distinction exactly.

**3.** (a) Item 3 (אַחֲוִי) begins with **אַ-** (aleph + patach), which is the **Aphel** causative prefix. The Haphel uses **הַ-** (heh + patach). The prefix consonant — aleph vs. heh — is the sole morphological difference. (b) The Aphel and Haphel are **functionally identical**: both express causation (H-stem function) using the same vowel patterns, the same conjugation structure, and the same semantic category. The difference is purely morphological: certain roots lexically select the Aphel prefix (אַ-) while others select the Haphel prefix (הַ-). The two are in complementary distribution — a given root uses one or the other. From the student's perspective, both forms mean "to cause [something] to happen / to make [someone] do X."

**4.** Root: **כתב** ("to write"). The three stems are: (a) **כְּתַב** — **Peal** perfect 3ms (simple active: "he wrote"); (b) **כְּתִיב** — **Peil** participle/adjective (simple passive: "written / it is written"); (c) **אִתְכְּתִב** — **Ithpeel** perfect 3ms (reflexive/passive: "it was written / was recorded"). The semantic relationship is: Peal = active ("he wrote"); Peil = stative passive result ("it stands written"); Ithpeel = the passive process ("it was [caused to be] written / it got written"). All three are G-stem derivatives (Ground stem) — the basic, intensive, and reflexive/passive are parallel in Hebrew (Qal / Qal passive / Niphal) but in Aramaic the Peil and Ithpeel serve those functions.

**5.** Item 12 (קַטֵּל) is the **Pael perfect 3ms** (the action itself: "he killed intensively / slaughtered"). Item 19 (מְקַטֵּל) is the **Pael participle ms** (the one performing the action: "the one who kills intensively / slaughterer"). The **single diagnostic morphological feature** for the Pael in both forms is the **dagesh forte in R2** (the doubled middle consonant ט = טּ). In the perfect (קַטֵּל), R2 has dagesh with patach under R1 and tsere under R2. In the participle (מְקַטֵּל), the מְ- derived-stem participle prefix is added, but R2 still has dagesh. The dagesh forte in R2 is the **defining marker** of the Pael (D stem) in every conjugation.
