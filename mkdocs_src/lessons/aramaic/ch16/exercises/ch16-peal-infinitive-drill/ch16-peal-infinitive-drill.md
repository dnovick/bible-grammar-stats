## Reflection Questions

1. Items 2 (לְמֵאמַר) and 3 (לְמֵיקַם) both have tsere (ֵ) under the mem of the מִ- prefix rather than the expected hireq (ִ). These come from two different weak root types. What causes the lengthening of the prefix vowel in each case, and are the causes the same or different?

2. Item 4 (לְמִנְפַּל) and item 7 (לְמִנְתַּן) both come from I-nun roots. In the Peal imperfect, I-nun roots assimilate their initial nun into the following consonant (e.g., יִפַּל from נפל). Why does the nun **not** assimilate in the infinitive construct? What morphological feature of the infinitive explains the retention of nun?

3. Item 8 (לְמִבְנֵא) ends in **אֵ-** (aleph + tsere). Item 6 (לְמֶעְבַּד) ends in a plain **ד**. Both are weak verbs but of different classes. What root class is each, and how does each class affect the shape of the infinitive?

4. Items 12 (מִשְׁמַע), 13 (מִכְתַּב), and 17 (מֶעְבַּד) appear without any prefix (no לְ or כְּ). What syntactic role(s) can a bare infinitive without a prefix serve? Give at least two possibilities.

5. Items 18–20 are Haphel infinitives. They share the pattern **לְהַ-** rather than **לְמִ-**. How does recognizing the Haphel prefix (הַ-) help you distinguish these from Peal infinitives at first glance? What is the significance of the Haphel stem in general (preview for Ch21)?

### Answers to Reflection Questions

**1.** In item 2 (לְמֵאמַר, root אמר), the tsere arises from the **I-aleph** root class. The aleph (א) of the root is a laryngeal/guttural that cannot easily follow the short hireq vowel of the מִ- prefix when it becomes quiescent. The hireq compensates by lengthening to tsere: מִ → מֵ. In item 3 (לְמֵיקַם, root קום), the tsere arises from the **hollow** root class. The middle waw of the root influences the prefix vowel, and the resulting form shows tsere under the mem with a yod appearing in place of the original waw. The two causes are **different**: one is the influence of an initial laryngeal radical; the other is the effect of a hollow (II-waw/yod) root on the vowel structure. However, both result in the same observable feature — tsere under the mem of the מִ- prefix — making them easy to confuse at first glance. The yod that follows in מֵיקַם (but not in מֵאמַר) is the key distinguishing signal.

**2.** The nun is retained in the infinitive because the מִ- prefix provides an **intervening syllable boundary** between the mem-prefix and the root. In the Peal imperfect, the prefix is a single consonant directly preceding the root: יִ + נפל → the nun finds itself between two consonants, which triggers assimilation (Dagesh Forte in the following consonant: יִפַּל). In the infinitive, however, the nun is the **first root consonant** immediately following the mem-prefix, and the syllable structure מִ-נְ-פַּל keeps the nun in an open syllable with a vocal shewa — there is no following geminated consonant, so no reason for assimilation. The morphological lesson: **assimilation of nun is triggered by imperfect prefix morphology (direct juxtaposition); the מִקְטַל infinitive pattern does not create that environment**.

**3.** Item 8 (לְמִבְנֵא) comes from root **בנה** — a **III-he root** (the third radical is originally ה, which historically is related to aleph-class consonants). In the infinitive, the III-he verb endings contract: the final he of the stem is replaced by aleph with tsere (אֵ-), giving the characteristic ending **-ֵא**. This is the III-he infinitive ending in Aramaic and is analogous to the way III-he roots behave in other conjugations (e.g., perfect 3ms בְּנָה, where the he appears as a mater). Item 6 (לְמֶעְבַּד) comes from root **עבד** — an **I-ayin root** (the first radical is ע, a guttural). The I-ayin root is a **strong** root by most classifications (it does not lose any consonant), but the initial ayin being a guttural affects the vowel of the מִ- prefix: the hireq (ִ) lowers to **seghol** (ֶ) before the guttural, giving מֶ- rather than מִ-. The final consonant is simply the third radical ד with no unusual ending — unlike the III-he forms.

**4.** A bare infinitive without a preposition prefix can serve three main roles: (a) **Subject of a clause** — e.g., "Writing is commanded" (מִכְתַּב מִפְקָד); (b) **Complement in apposition to a noun** — standing in apposition to a previously mentioned verbal noun or action; (c) **Epexegetical infinitive** — clarifying or expanding on a preceding noun or pronoun ("the decree, namely the writing of it…"). In the biblical corpus, bare infinitives without prefix most commonly appear in the construct relationship (the infinitive governs a following genitive noun: "the hearing of the word" = מִשְׁמַע מִלְּתָא), where the infinitive functions as the head noun of a construct chain.

**5.** The Haphel infinitive uses the prefix pattern **הַ-** (he + patach), which is the Haphel stem marker. When לְ is prepended, the whole prefix is **לְהַ-**, giving forms like לְהַחֲוָיָה. The Peal infinitive pattern is **לְמִ-**. Seeing **לְהַ-** rather than **לְמִ-** immediately signals a derived stem — specifically Haphel (the Aramaic causative, equivalent to Hebrew Hiphil). The significance of the Haphel stem (previewing Ch21) is that it makes an intransitive verb transitive, or adds a causative nuance to a transitive verb: Peal יְדַע "to know" → Haphel הוֹדַע "to make known, to inform"; Peal חזה "to see" → Haphel הַחֲוָה "to show, to make [someone] see." Recognizing the לְהַ-/לְהוֹ- prefix pattern now prepares you to identify these forms throughout Daniel and Ezra before the full Haphel chapter is reached.
